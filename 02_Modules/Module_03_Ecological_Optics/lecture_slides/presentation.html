Lecture 04

Locomotion I

Empirical studies of some of Gibson’s rules for locomotion

Modes of Locomotion

Visual perception makes locomotion truly useful because we can orient to distal objects.

Ecological Context

  • Ambient energy arrays (optic array) have structure.
  • Structure is information available to be detected.
  • Detection occurs directly without internal mediational states.
  • Optic flow fields are used for control without high-level storage.

Gibson's Rules

Visual Guidance Rules

  • To be still: Minimize change in the optic flow.
  • To move forward/backward: Produce global inflow/outflow.
  • To move to a target: Put focus of expansion (FOE) on target.
  • To change direction: Shift the location of the FOE.
  • Approach without collision: Keep rate of expansion steady.

Moving Room Paradigm

Lee & Lishman (1974)

Visual proprioception vs. Mechanical proprioception. Vision typically overrides other sources.

Stoffregen (1985)

Peripheral vs. Central Flow

Conclusion: Peripheral flow has a stronger influence on perceived egomotion than central flow.

Warren (1988)

Perceiving Heading

Can people determine locomotion direction based on optic flow patterns? Retinal flow vs. Ambient optic flow.

Warren et al. (2001)

FOE vs. Egocentric Strategy

  • Optic Flow Strategy: Predicts linear trajectories.
  • Egocentric Strategy: Predicts curved trajectories.

Results favored the optic flow hypothesis, though egocentric direction still matters.

Time-to-Contact (Tau)

Animals can control approach by using a particular kind of information: Tau (τ).

  • Direct perception: No need to judge absolute distance or speed.
  • Tau is the inverse of the rate of expansion of a closed optical contour.

Conclusion

Perception-Action Loop

Optic flow demonstrates the importance of activity for perception. It is not defined in the absence of observer motion.
