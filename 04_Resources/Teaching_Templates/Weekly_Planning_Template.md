## üéØ Design Principle: Experience ‚Üí Theory ‚Üí Application Cycle

The weekly rhythm creates a **feedback loop** between embodied experience and conceptual understanding:

```
Week N Tuesday (Activity Day):
   Experience phenomenon ‚Üí Generate questions
      ‚Üì
Week N Thursday (Discussion Day):
   Reading explains phenomenon ‚Üí Deepen understanding
      ‚Üì
Week N+1 Tuesday (Activity Day):
   Apply concepts ‚Üí Test predictions ‚Üí New questions
```

**Result:** Students cycle between doing and thinking, building intuition and theory together.

## üîÑ Common Weekly Patterns (Choose Based on Content)

### **Pattern A: Activity ‚Üí Discussion (Recommended for New Concepts)**

**Tuesday (Activity Day):** Experience phenomenon ‚Üí Generate confusion/questions
**Thursday (Discussion Day):** Reading resolves confusion ‚Üí Deepen theory

**Example:**
- **Tue:** Ebbinghaus demo ‚Üí "How did my hand know the real size?"
- **Thu:** Blau & Wagman Ch 3 (Axioms) ‚Üí "Direct perception of affordances"

**When to Use:**
- Introducing counterintuitive concepts (e.g., direct perception, coordination dynamics)
- Building from concrete to abstract
- High student engagement needed

---

### **Pattern B: Discussion ‚Üí Activity (Good for Testing Theory)**

**Tuesday (Discussion Day):** Reading introduces theory ‚Üí Generate predictions
**Thursday (Activity Day):** Demo tests predictions ‚Üí Apply theory

**Example:**
- **Tue:** Gibson Ch 5 (Optic Flow) ‚Üí "Tau specifies time-to-contact"
- **Thu:** Optic flow demo ‚Üí "Can we detect tau? Does it guide braking?"

**When to Use:**
- Theory is accessible from reading alone
- Demo serves as test/application of theory
- Consolidating prior learning

---

### **Pattern C: Dual Activities (Rare, for Complex Topics)**

**Tuesday (Activity 1):** Baseline demo
**Thursday (Activity 2):** Variation or extension

**Example:**
- **Tue:** Finger oscillation (in-phase vs anti-phase)
- **Thu:** Add external pacing ‚Üí Observe phase transitions

**When to Use:**
- Complex phenomena requiring multiple exposures
- Building toward a cumulative insight
- Mid-semester when students have strong conceptual foundation

**Note:** Assign reading between sessions to provide theoretical scaffolding.

---

### **Pattern D: Discussion ‚Üí Discussion (Theory-Heavy Weeks)**

**Tuesday (Discussion Day):** Foundational reading
**Thursday (Discussion Day):** Application or critique reading

**Example:**
- **Tue:** Blau & Wagman Ch 3 (9 Axioms)
- **Thu:** Blau & Wagman Ch 4 (Planimeter metaphor)

**When to Use:**
- Building theoretical foundation early in semester
- Review/synthesis weeks before exams
- Preparing for major conceptual shift

**Warning:** Risk of passivity. Use Discussion Day Protocol rigorously (student questions, active engagement).

## üóìÔ∏è Semester-Level Planning: Mapping Patterns to Arc

### Early Semester (Weeks 1-4): Build Intuition
**Dominant Pattern:** Activity ‚Üí Discussion
**Why:** Students need embodied experience to grasp counterintuitive concepts (direct perception, ecological optics).

**Example:**
- **Week 1:** Assumptions demo ‚Üí Reading on Standard Story
- **Week 2:** Optic flow demo ‚Üí Reading on tau
- **Week 3:** Ebbinghaus ‚Üí Reading on Axioms

---

### Mid-Semester (Weeks 5-9): Deepen Theory
**Dominant Pattern:** Discussion ‚Üí Activity
**Why:** Students can now predict phenomena from theory; demos test their understanding.

**Example:**
- **Week 5:** Reading on affordances ‚Üí Stair-climbing demo
- **Week 6:** Reading on dynamic touch ‚Üí Wielding demo
- **Week 7:** Reading on coordination ‚Üí Finger oscillation

---

### Late Semester (Weeks 10-14): Synthesis & Application
**Dominant Pattern:** Discussion ‚Üí Discussion or Student-Led Activities
**Why:** Students integrate across topics; less instructor-led demo, more student application.

**Example:**
- **Week 10:** Debate reading (Does direct perception fail for X?)
- **Week 11:** Student-designed demo (groups propose and run)
- **Week 12:** Capstone discussion (Synthesis across course)

## üìã Master Planning Checklist (Use at Semester Start)

### Step 1: Identify Core Concepts (One per week)
- [ ] List 12-14 core concepts for the semester
- [ ] Sequence from concrete ‚Üí abstract or simple ‚Üí complex

### Step 2: Match Concepts to Demos & Readings
- [ ] For each concept, identify:
  - **Demo:** What embodied experience reveals this?
  - **Reading:** What text explains this?

### Step 3: Assign Weekly Pattern
- [ ] For each week, choose pattern (A, B, C, or D)
- [ ] Early semester: Favor Pattern A (Activity ‚Üí Discussion)
- [ ] Mid-semester: Favor Pattern B (Discussion ‚Üí Activity)
- [ ] Late semester: Favor Pattern D or student-led

### Step 4: Build Week-by-Week Plan
- [ ] Use Weekly Planning Template for each week
- [ ] Ensure pre-class tasks are distributed (not all weeks heavy)
- [ ] Plan "light weeks" before exams or after intense demos

### Step 5: Create Callbacks & Threads
- [ ] Each week should reference prior weeks ("Remember the Ebbinghaus demo?")
- [ ] Identify 2-3 "big ideas" that recur across semester
- [ ] Plan cumulative synthesis discussions (Week 7, Week 14)

## üß∞ Quick Reference: Protocol Selection Guide

| Situation | Use This Protocol |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Students need to experience phenomenon first** | Activity Day Protocol |
| **Reading introduces a testable theory** | Discussion Day Protocol (with prediction task) |
| **Concept is highly abstract (e.g., "direct perception")** | Activity Day first, then Discussion Day |
| **Students have misconceptions to surface** | Discussion Day with pre-class questions |
| **Consolidating prior learning** | Activity Day (apply theory) or Discussion Day (synthesize readings) |
| **Building toward complex synthesis** | Dual Discussion Days or Discussion ‚Üí Activity |

#### **Post-Week Reflection (Friday, Jan 30)**

**What Worked:**
- Ebbinghaus demo landed well; students grasped (literally!) the perception-action dissociation.
- Discussion questions were generative; Q3 (Homunculus) sparked debate.
- Student-submitted questions on optic flow were thoughtful (especially "How does tau work if you're accelerating?").

**What Needs Adjustment:**
- "Tau" is still fuzzy for some students. Plan callback/reteach on Feb 3.
- Thursday's optic flow video was too fast; need slower version or ability to pause/replay.

**Callback for Next Week:**
- Revisit tau with a demo (e.g., catching a ball, braking simulation).
- Connect tau to affordances (graspability from Week 3).

## üéì Final Note: These Are Living Documents

**Your teaching will evolve:**
- First iteration: Follow protocols closely.
- Mid-semester: Adapt based on student needs (more scaffolding? Less?).
- Next semester: Refine based on what worked.

**Update these templates:**
- Add "What Worked" and "What Failed" notes to protocols.
- Share successful variants with future you (or colleagues).

**Trust the process:**
- Discussion Days feel risky (what if students don't submit good questions?). They will. And even "bad" questions reveal useful confusion.
- Activity Days feel chaotic (what if the demo fails?). It sometimes will. Embrace it as data.

**The goal:** Students who think like ecological psychologists‚Äîasking questions, testing predictions, connecting theory to lived experience. These protocols support that goal.
