Overview

Today you will complete two experiments that investigate the relationship between conscious perception and motor control:

  1. Activity 1: Perception Task - Does the illusion work?
  2. Activity 2: Fitts Tapping Task - Does the illusion affect continuous movement?

The Central Question: If your brain builds a “mental picture” to guide both perception and action, and that picture is wrong (illusion), what happens when you try to act?

Keep this packet throughout class. You will use your observations during the discussion section.

ACTIVITY 1: Perception Task (Computerized Susceptibility Test)

Reference: Ebbinghaus (1902); Titchener (1901)

1) The Computerized Ebbinghaus Test

You will complete a computerized susceptibility test that measures how strongly the Ebbinghaus illusion affects your perception.

The Test: - You will see multiple trials with two circles (left and right) - Each circle may be surrounded by small circles, large circles, or no circles - The central circles (orange/red) may be the same size or different sizes - Your task: Judge which central circle appears larger - Press F if the left circle looks bigger - Press J if the right circle looks bigger - Press Space if they look equal

At the end: The computer will calculate your Susceptibility Score - the percentage of trials where the surrounding context (small vs. large inducers) influenced your judgment, even when the central circles were physically the same size.

ACTIVITY 2: Oscillatory Fitts Task

Reference: Fitts (1954); Franz et al. (2000) - Planning vs. control dissociation

1) Setup: The Fitts Tapping Task

You will perform rapid alternating movements between two target circles under different Ebbinghaus context conditions.

The Task: - Start with your finger/stylus on one target circle - Move as quickly and accurately as possible to the other target circle - Alternate back and forth between the two targets - Complete 10 oscillations (20 taps total: 10 on each target)

Important: In each condition, BOTH targets have the SAME surrounding context (not different).

Three Context Conditions: - Condition A (No Context): Both targets are plain circles (no surrounding circles) - Condition B (Large Context): Both targets surrounded by large circles (targets should look smaller) - Condition C (Small Context): Both targets surrounded by small circles (targets should look larger)

Two Size Blocks: - Block 1: Both targets are 2 cm diameter - Block 2: Both targets are 3 cm diameter

Reality: The targets are the physical size they are! The surrounding context creates an illusion of size difference, but doesn’t change the actual target dimensions.

2) Hypothesis

From Activity 1, you know: Targets surrounded by small circles look larger than targets surrounded by large circles.

Fitt’s Law suggests that movement time and accuracy of tapping between targets is affected by target size, where smaller targets are likely to be more difficult to(show increased movement time and tapping errors).

Question: Will the surrounding context affect your movement performance?

Formulate hypotheses for:

Movement Time (MT)

☐ Be slowest in Condition B (large context - targets look smaller/harder to hit) ☐ Be slowest in Condition C (small context - targets look larger/easier to hit) ☐ Be fastest in Condition A (no context - no illusion) ☐ Be the same across all conditions (context won’t affect movement time) ☐ Something else: _________________________________________________________________

Why? (What information controls your movement speed?)

Accuracy (Errors)

When tapping under different context conditions, I will make:

☐ More errors in Condition B (large context - targets look smaller) ☐ More errors in Condition C (small context - targets look larger) ☐ More errors in Condition A (no context baseline) ☐ Equal errors across all conditions (context won’t affect accuracy) ☐ Something else: _________________________________________________________________

Why? (What information controls your movement accuracy?)

3) Procedure

Materials: - Three sets of tapping surfaces, each with two targets separated by 15 cm (center-to-center): - Set A: No surrounding circles (plain targets) - Set B: Both targets surrounded by large circles - Set C: Both targets surrounded by small circles - Two target sizes: 2 cm and 3 cm diameter - Stylus or finger for tapping - Stopwatch

Instructions:

You will complete 6 conditions (3 contexts × 2 sizes), with 3 trials per condition:

  1. Block 1 (2 cm targets):
    • Condition A (No Context): 3 trials
    • Condition B (Large Context): 3 trials
    • Condition C (Small Context): 3 trials
  2. Block 2 (3 cm targets):
    • Condition A (No Context): 3 trials
    • Condition B (Large Context): 3 trials
    • Condition C (Small Context): 3 trials

For each trial: 1. Starting Position: Place your stylus/finger on left target. 2. Command “Go!”: Begin alternating as quickly and accurately as possible. 3. Complete 10 oscillations (Left→Right→Left→Right… for a total of 20 taps). 4. Partner Records: - Movement Time (MT): Total time to complete 10 oscillations (use stopwatch) - Errors: Number of taps that miss the target circle (count only clear misses)

2) Three Possible Explanations

Explanation 1: Visual Perception Action uses the same information as conscious perception (predicts illusion affects action)

Explanation 2: Two Visual Systems One system for perception (fooled by illusion), one for action (not fooled)

Explanation 3: Task-Specific Information Different tasks detect different information sources

3) Connecting to Theory

The Standard Story would say: Your brain builds a mental representation of the target. If that representation is wrong (illusion), your movements should always be affected too.

The Ecological Approach (Blau & Wagman) would say:

Think about: - What is the difference between perceiving for judgment vs. perceiving for action? - Could your eyes and your movement system be detecting different higher-order variables? - What information does the visual system detect for judgment vs. for guiding continuous action?

6) Extension: Fitts’ Law

Fitts’ Law (1954): Movement Time = a + b × log₂(2A/W)

Where: - A = Amplitude (distance to target) - W = Width (size of target) - Index of Difficulty (ID) = log₂(2A/W)

If the illusion affected movement, what should happen?

(Hint: If Target A looked smaller, what would happen to W in the formula?)
